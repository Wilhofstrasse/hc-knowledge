3. I wrote 8 of 15 emergency playbooks

Comment on §3. I wrote 8 of 15 emergency playbooks

For each thing that could go wrong, what to do — step by step, who to call, how to roll back. All the day-one critical ones are done:

  • If our encryption system goes down (consultants can't open or save decks)
  • If client data ever leaks across tenants (the worst-case scenario — multi-channel page, lawyers, data-privacy officer)
  • If the audit log gets too far behind (the 5-minute alarm)
  • If a deck gets corrupted (replay from the audit log)
  • If our login system (Microsoft 365) is down
  • If our long-term storage was set up wrong (this one is unrecoverable-by-design — the playbook is prevention-first)
  • If our AI provider runs out of capacity (auto-failover to the other provider)
  • If one client's content shows up in another client's deck (engagement-confidentiality breach)

The remaining 7 playbooks are for later phases (regulated-tenant scenarios, product-wide outages, AI model retirement) — not blocking anything right now.

5. I found a missing piece — your hunch was right

Comment on §5. I found a missing piece — your hunch was right

When you mentioned the prompting video and asked "are we missing the prompting module?", I sent it to three reviewers (two helper AIs and the second AI we use). All three said: yes, real gap.

What's missing: the architecture has a connector for "which AI prompt produced this answer?" — but the thing it connects to (a prompt registry) doesn't exist yet. For regulated clients, when an investigator asks "show me the exact prompt behind this misleading number on slide 17 in 2030", we'd have nothing to show.

Two more reasons to build it: (a) saves about $360,000/year at full scale because of how AI providers price repeated content (we'd be re-sending the same boring intro to the AI on every call instead of using their built-in cache); (b) without it, every part of the tool would invent its own way of writing prompts, and they'd drift apart over time.

I drafted the proposal and put it in the holding folder. Three reviewers all said GREEN (no problems). Waiting for your sign-off.

What this is. A multi-tenant PowerPoint add-in for consulting-grade decks. Consultants click a shape, the AI fills it with text grounded in real sources, every claim has a traffic-light, every action is logged for audit. Built for regulated industries — banking, insurance, asset management, pharma — where "show me where this number came from" is not optional.

Why this page exists. The full architecture has 10 invariants, 6 primitives, 6 user flows, and ~200 pages of locked specs across multiple files. That's right for engineers and Codex. Comment on §🖼️ Imagery LOCKED v2.1

All non-text visual content the AI fetches or proposes — cover photos, competitor/peer-firm logos for peer analysis, country flags + sector icons for PESTEL slides, peer-map firm icons. One module, broad scope.

6-tier picker for cover photos: user-upload → IR press kit → H&C curated library → licensed stock (Shutterstock + Getty) → AI-gen (illustrative only, NEVER fake real-firm building photos) → H&C placeholder.

Out of scope: employee/people photos (privacy + GDPR — manual upload only, not AI-fetched).

🔍 Grounding LOCKED v2.1 v2.1.1 pending

Comment on §🔍 Grounding LOCKED v2.1 v2.1.1 pending

The proof layer for every textual statement in the deck. Sources get ingested at deck open, retrieved via search, fed to the AI as grounded prompts, validated by structured-output schemas + semantic entailment, surfaced as traffic-lights + paragraph-level citations.

Filipe's model-agnostic feedback (#3) reshapes this: the spine is the universal citation envelope + entailment validator, NOT any one provider's API. Anthropic / Google / OpenAI become adapter optimizations.

Open: 15 Q-GR defaults, semantic entailment NLI eval set, multi-vendor cost attribution.
